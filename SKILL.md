# TrustedTech Cold Email

## Core Objective

Create direct outreach that produces meaningful business conversations, qualified meetings, pipeline creation, opportunity creation, and seller adoption.

Do not optimize for vanity engagement, curiosity replies, clicks, or low-intent responses unless the user explicitly asks for nurture copy.

## Default Output Standards

- Keep most direct outreach emails around 50-75 words unless the user asks otherwise.
- Use one CTA.
- Make every CTA a direct question.
- Make the CTA easy to answer with yes, sure, send it over, or a referral.
- Keep the email easy to scan on mobile.
- Use direct, calm, operational language.
- Do not use em dashes.
- Do not use buzzwords, hype, exaggerated urgency, or fluffy thought leadership.
- Do not overexplain the offer.
- Do not make the meeting the product. The outcome is the product.

## TrustedTech Voice

Write like an operationally literate colleague, not a marketing campaign.

Sound human, direct, consultative, calm, specific, useful, and executive-friendly.

Avoid corporate jargon, fake empathy, overly polished copy, cleverness for its own sake, aggressive sales language, obvious scraped-data language, and vendor-heavy phrasing.

## Core Email Structure

Use this structure by default:

1. Operational relevance or market observation
2. Specific pain, capability gap, or timing pressure
3. Tangible outcome or deliverable
4. Low-friction direct question

The email should answer quickly:

- Why this matters now
- What useful outcome the recipient could get
- What simple action they can take next

## Diagnostic Question Framework

When useful, open with a diagnostic question that makes the recipient think, "I'm not sure."

Good diagnostic questions ask whether the buyer has a specific capability, answer, or visibility gap.

Examples:

- Do you have a clear side-by-side view of current VMware costs versus projected Azure operating costs?
- Can your team already tell which workloads would create the most migration cost or complexity?
- Do you already know where Azure could reduce spend versus where it would just shift the cost?

Avoid generic questions:

- Are you looking to save money?
- Are you thinking about cloud migration?
- Do you want to modernize your infrastructure?

## Offer Rules

The offer must feel like a useful business deliverable, not just more information.

Strong offer types:

- Cost comparison
- Savings report
- Readiness review
- Exposure analysis
- Findings report
- Gap review
- Roadmap
- Prioritization review
- Optimization report

Weak offer types:

- More information
- A quick chat
- Compare notes
- Learn more
- A demo
- General thought leadership

## CTA Rules

Prefer CTAs that ask whether the useful outcome would be worth seeing.

Strong CTAs:

- Would an Azure cost comparison be useful?
- Would it be helpful to see what that comparison could look like for {{account.name}}?
- Would it be useful if our team prepared one for {{account.name}}?
- Would this be useful, or is there someone else on your team who handles infrastructure planning?

Use a disarming CTA pattern when appropriate:

I'm sure you're getting plenty of outreach around [topic] right now, but if this is something your team is evaluating, would [specific outcome] be useful?

Avoid:

- Do you have 15 minutes?
- Can we schedule a call?
- Would you be open to learning more?
- Happy to discuss.
- Let me know.

## Programmatic Personalization Guidance

Make programmatic personalization feel natural and less obvious.

Avoid revealing the data source or mechanism:

- {{account.name}} shows up as a VMware environment.
- I saw you use Citrix.
- We noticed your company has [technology].

Prefer market or peer-context framing:

- We're speaking with a small group of infrastructure leaders about VMware renewal planning following the Broadcom changes.
- A lot of teams are comparing VMware costs against Azure before renewal decisions get locked in.
- We're gathering input from infrastructure leaders navigating VMware renewal and modernization decisions this year.

Use personalization only when it feels naturally human, not scraped.

## Incentive Guidance

When an incentive is used, frame it as a thank-you for participation and ensure the outreach remains transparent and compliant.

Good framing:

- We're offering a $100 thank-you for infrastructure leaders open to sharing how they're approaching VMware renewal planning.

Avoid framing the incentive as payment for a meeting.

## Sequence Rules

When writing a sequence:

- Keep each email able to stand alone.
- Keep the first email focused on the strongest pain and offer.
- Use the second email to deepen the operational tension or shift the angle.
- Avoid repeating the same email with different words.
- If the user asks for document format, use clear sequence documentation with name, subject, body, and day.

## VMware Detected Campaign Playbook

Use when targeting accounts with VMware detected.

Core tension:

Organizations are reevaluating VMware strategy before renewal pressure and pricing changes force decisions.

Strong angles:

- VMware renewal planning after Broadcom changes
- Cost visibility before decisions lock in
- Comparing VMware costs against Azure
- Side-by-side Azure cost comparison
- Immediate savings and optimization opportunities

Best offer:

Azure cost comparison.

Best positioning:

- Invite the recipient into a small group of infrastructure leaders.
- Emphasize cost visibility and planning clarity.
- Avoid directly saying the account was scraped as a VMware environment.

Example:

Hi {{first_name}},

I wanted to invite you to be part of a small group of infrastructure leaders we're speaking with about VMware renewal planning following the Broadcom changes.

For participating organizations, we're preparing side-by-side Azure cost comparisons to help identify potential savings and modernization opportunities before renewal decisions get locked in.

I'm sure you're getting plenty of outreach around VMware right now, but if this is something your team is evaluating, would an Azure cost comparison be useful?

Best,
{{sender.first_name}}

## Windows Server / SQL EOL Campaign Playbook

Use when targeting Windows Server or SQL Server end-of-life signals.

Core tension:

Unsupported or aging workloads create security, compliance, cost, and modernization pressure.

Strong angles:

- Avoid rushed migration decisions
- Understand what Azure modernization could cost
- Identify workloads creating risk or unnecessary cost
- Plan modernization before deadlines force action

Best offer:

Server modernization cost review or Azure migration cost comparison.

## Citrix Detected Campaign Playbook

Use Citrix as a signal only when the angle includes operational overhead, VDI cost, Azure Virtual Desktop, Microsoft-native consolidation, or endpoint modernization.

Do not simply say the company uses Citrix.

Strong angles:

- VDI cost visibility
- Reducing management overhead
- Evaluating Azure Virtual Desktop or Microsoft-native alternatives
- Simplifying remote workforce infrastructure

Best offer:

VDI modernization cost comparison or Azure Virtual Desktop planning review.

## Cloud Migration Intent Campaign Playbook

Use when the signal is behavioral intent around cloud migration.

Core tension:

The team may be evaluating cloud migration options but lacks clarity on cost, sequencing, or implementation complexity.

Strong angles:

- Cost clarity before migration decisions
- Avoiding lift-and-shift waste
- Identifying migration blockers
- Prioritizing workloads

Best offer:

Azure migration cost analysis or migration readiness snapshot.

## Server Migration Intent Campaign Playbook

Use when the signal is server migration intent.

Core tension:

The team may be modernizing aging infrastructure and needs to understand cost, risk, and sequencing.

Strong angles:

- Migration planning before infrastructure deadlines
- Azure cost comparison
- Avoiding unnecessary migration complexity
- Identifying optimization opportunities

Best offer:

Server migration cost comparison or Azure modernization review.

## Quality Checklist

Before finalizing, verify:

- Does the email feel like a person wrote it?
- Is the useful outcome clear within 10 seconds?
- Is the CTA one direct question?
- Is the CTA lower friction than booking a meeting?
- Does the offer feel valuable?
- Is the email free of obvious scraped-data language?
- Is the copy 75 words or less if requested?
- Does it create a meaningful sales conversation rather than a curiosity reply?
- Would a seller trust the reply quality?
